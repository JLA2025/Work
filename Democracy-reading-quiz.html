Section 2: Argentina's Complex Democratic Journey

Read about Argentina's democratic development:

Argentina became independent of Spanish colonial rule in 1810, though as a settler colony this independence is considered hollow by many indigenous people who point to colonial continuities. In the nineteenth century, elite political struggles focused on nation-building. In the twentieth century, politics revolved around struggles for power, including the demand for political inclusion and greater social equality.

Repudiation of these demands often resulted in military coups, of which Argentina suffered no fewer than five: 1930, 1943, 1955, 1966, 1976. However, Argentina's return to electoral democracy in 1983, in which human rights demands played a crucial role, has endured to today.

The democracies that rule Latin America today, incomplete and troubled as they are, are nevertheless a source of pride for most Latin Americans and have certainly been hard won.

Levelled:

Argentina became independent from Spain in 1810. However, many native people say this independence was not real because Spanish ideas and control continued. In the 1800s, important people fought about how to build the nation. In the 1900s, politics was about power struggles. People wanted to be included in politics and wanted more equal treatment.

When the government refused these demands, the military often took control of the country. This happened five times: in 1930, 1943, 1955, 1966, and 1976. But in 1983, Argentina became a democracy again. People who wanted human rights helped make this happen. Argentina has been a democracy since then.

The democracies in Latin America today are not perfect and have problems. But most Latin Americans are proud of them because they were difficult to achieve.

Section 3: The Nature of Populism

Read about populism as a political phenomenon:

Populism is a form of government that combines electoral democracy with a cult of personality. Populism does not focus on a particular political agenda (populists can be left or right wing) but rather on a type of political relationship between a leader and the people.

The populist leader often has a charismatic persona and 'performs' powerfulness through rhetoric and action. Gender roles are important to this image too because powerfulness, control and competence are associated with masculinity. Like many populist leaders, populists portray themselves as 'men of action' who won't let bureaucracy or elite interests stand in the way of helping the poor.

In some ways, populism seems anti-democratic because it empowers a single charismatic leader and solidifies hierarchies like class and gender, rather than dismantling them. However, leaders are nothing without the mass of people to support them, and examples of successful populism are driven by the demands and desires of people who feel overlooked, disadvantaged and disparaged.

Levelled:

Populism is a type of government that mixes democracy with strong worship of a leader. Populism is not about specific political ideas (populist leaders can be left-wing or right-wing). Instead, it is about the special relationship between a leader and the people.

Populist leaders are usually very attractive and charming. They show their power through speaking and actions. Gender roles are important here because people connect power, control and ability with being masculine. Populist leaders show themselves as 'men of action' who will not let government rules or rich people stop them from helping poor people.

In some ways, populism seems anti-democratic because it gives power to one charming leader. It makes differences between social classes and men and women stronger, instead of removing these differences. However, leaders need many people to support them. Successful populism happens when people who feel ignored, disadvantaged and looked down upon want change.

Section 4: The Peróns and Gender Roles in Populism

Read about Juan and Eva Perón:

Juan Perón had a magnetic personality and gave brilliant speeches. He 'performed' his connection to ordinary people by taking off his jacket and rolling up his sleeves, by playing sports and emphasising his recent immigrant past. He made use of a masculine image as a father figure, a role that makes dominance seem natural, protective and legitimate.

His paternalistic and macho persona was reinforced by the femininity of his wife, Eva Duarte. Evita was a hugely charismatic person in her own right, whose greatest part as an actress was to 'perform' her roles of humble provincial girl, glamorous fashionista and mother of the poor. She was simultaneously ordinary and extraordinary, conveying the idea that she knew how hard a life of poverty could be, but now occupied a place of power from where she could 'change the world'.

Evita also performed the role of dutiful wife, declaring her unconditional support for her husband and urging the working class to trust in Perón, the father-figure, as she did. By playing up to these idealised gender roles, Juan and Eva Perón legitimised their powerfulness and won the hearts of the people.

Section 5: Clientelism - Benefits as Political Favours

Read about clientelism in Argentine politics:

Perón distributed national wealth more evenly and raised poor people's standard of living. However, these state welfare benefits were often channelled through the Peronist party machine. Benefits are not so much 'rights', then, as 'politicised favours'.

Known as clientelism, this system channels goods, services, opportunities, money, benefits and jobs through the Peronist patron–client networks. The party has a network of activists who distribute social welfare and benefits through grassroots offices that act as community centres. They host pensioners' lunches and weekend barbecues as well as party meetings.

While distributing such welfare has a tangible and positive impact on poor people's lives, it undermines a core principle of democracy – equality – because benefits are not distributed evenly to everyone because they are citizens (rights), but unevenly and to a few because they are Peronist supporters (favours).

Levelled:

Perón shared the country's wealth more equally and improved poor people's lives. However, these government benefits were often given through the Peronist party organisation. So benefits were not really 'rights' for all people, but 'political favours' for some people.

This system is called clientelism. It gives goods, services, opportunities, money, benefits and jobs through Peronist networks. The party has workers who give out social welfare and benefits through local offices that work like community centres. They organise lunches for old people and weekend barbecues, as well as party meetings.

Giving out welfare like this helps poor people's lives in real ways. But it damages an important principle of democracy – equality. This is because benefits are not given equally to everyone because they are citizens (rights). Instead, they are given unequally and only to some people because they support the Peronist party (favours).

Section 6: The Madres de la Plaza de Mayo

Read about Argentina's human rights movement:

The last military dictatorship of 1976–83 was one of the most brutal in Latin America, ordering the abduction, murder and disappearance of around 30,000 people whom it understood to be enemies of the Argentine nation. The climate of fear was so intense that very few spoke out against the dictatorship, but the most vocal of those were the Madres de la Plaza de Mayo.

The Madres are the mothers of the young people who were 'disappeared' by the military government. They met once a week in Buenos Aires' main square where the presidential palace, cathedral and government ministries are located. Here they walked around the central obelisk in quiet yet powerful condemnation of the military government.

Their third significant contribution to democracy in Argentina was to demonstrate that social movements of ordinary people could transform political life. They brought new political actors – and therefore new ideas – to the fore and therefore democratised the political stage.

Levelled:

From 1976 to 1983, Argentina had a very cruel military government. This government kidnapped, killed, and made about 30,000 people disappear because they thought these people were enemies. People were very scared, so most people did not speak against the military government. But the Madres (Mothers) de la Plaza de Mayo were the loudest protesters.

The Madres were mothers whose children had 'disappeared' because of the military government. Every week, they met in the main square of Buenos Aires. This square has the president's house, the main church, and government buildings. The mothers walked around a monument in the square to protest against the military government in a quiet but strong way.

The Madres made an important contribution to democracy in Argentina. They showed that ordinary people in social movements could change political life. They brought new political people and new ideas into politics. This made politics more democratic.

Section 9: Challenging Western-Centric Views of Democracy

Read about different perspectives on democracy:

One of the key assumptions buried deep within conventional understandings of democracy is that the best democracies are in the West. This is the message portrayed in conventional textbooks, which start their thinking about democracy from political systems in the USA, the UK and other European countries and use these as the yardstick by which to measure how good or deficient a democracy is.

This means that Western democracies are already set-up to 'succeed' by these criteria while the others are pre-destined to fail or are portrayed as playing catch-up. The word 'democratic' is not value-neutral. It has a silent tag 'good' attached to it, and the label 'non-democratic' is therefore silently tagged with the word 'bad' or at least 'dubious'.

Beginning our thinking from Argentina and not the USA disrupts our assumptions about what is 'normal' practice in a democracy and it questions the super-valuation of political practice in 'developed' democracies. It helps to break through the presumption that the USA has got democracy 'right' and that others simply need to follow suit.

Section 10: Democratising Our Understanding of Democracy

Read the author's conclusion:

Is democracy a good idea, then? In the end, for me, the answer to the question is yes. I think that democracy seeks to enact political ideals that I find crucial and that are shared by a wide range of cultures and societies. However, I think that we need to democratise our understanding of democracy.

One of the key problems with what we call democracy is the way that it has been hitched to the political systems of powerful countries, wrapped up in a moral lesson about the way that societies ought to be. One of the things that we can do when we are thinking about democracy, then, is to make these value judgements visible and problematise them.

There is no one way to be democratic, and in my view a plurality of ideas, voices, and cultural understandings can enrich all our democracies, even those that are longest established. If we want to look for ways to broaden or deepen democracy, then, we should look to the social realm and imagine democracy as not just a way to choose decision-makers but a way of life.
